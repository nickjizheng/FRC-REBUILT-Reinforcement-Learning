## Per-camera RL analysis

### 1. onboard_intake — front cam, pitched down
- **Observes:** fuel balls on the floor from ~0.4 m out to ~6 m (30 px diameter at 2 m,
  ~12 px at 5 m — trivially segmentable: saturated yellow on gray floor), the near field
  geometry (bump edge, trench, hub base), and the hub funnel when facing it.
- **Blind to:** the hub opening while close (pitched down), balls inside the intake
  dead-zone (< ~0.4 m, below FOV), everything behind/level with the robot, own hopper
  contents.
- **Occlusion failures:** balls behind BUMPS/TRENCH structures and behind the hub base;
  balls directly behind another robot; when the robot noses up a bump the whole view
  tilts to sky.
- **Sim-to-real:** excellent — matches a real front-mounted intake cam. Ball detection
  transfers with basic domain randomization (the real fuel is also a saturated ball).

### 2. onboard_shooter — rear-top cam by the muzzle, pitched slightly up
- **Observes:** the hub opening dead-center whenever the robot is aimed (the funnel slot
  is ~100 px wide at 4.5 m — plenty for an aim-error signal), wall/tower skyline for
  coarse localization, and — because it sits high — still sees floor balls beyond ~1.5 m.
  Part of the robot's own shooter structure is visible at frame bottom = free
  proprioceptive anchor.
- **Blind to:** balls closer than ~1.5 m (hidden by own chassis), anything behind.
- **Occlusion failures:** hub hidden by TOWER structures from far corners; opposing
  robot parked in the line of sight; view degenerates to featureless wall when facing
  the short walls.
- **Sim-to-real:** excellent — this is exactly a Limelight-style aiming camera. The
  36h11 tags around the field sit in this view's band; at 512²/74° a 0.206 m tag decodes
  reliably only inside ~3 m (needs ≥ ~25 px across) — use 640-960 px or tag-corner
  regression for longer-range tag pose.

### 3. chase — 1.6 m back, 1.0 m up, 15° down
- **Observes:** robot pose *and* local context in one frame (chassis, intake heading,
  balls, hub). Great gradient signal early in training because the ego-body is visible.
- **Occlusion failures (visible in the render):** the robot itself masks the balls right
  in front of the intake — precisely the most decision-relevant pixels; camera clips
  into walls/towers when the robot backs up (virtual boom leaves the field volume).
- **Sim-to-real:** not physically realizable on the real robot. Use only as a
  debug/eval-video camera or for privileged distillation experiments.

### 4. overhead_field — god view, z = 14 m
- **Observes:** everything at once: all fuel (bright ~4-5 px dots — marginal but
  segmentable by color), both hubs, bumps, trenches, robot pose (dark rectangle), full
  arena topology. The single richest observation for *learning speed*.
- **Occlusion failures:** balls under the hub funnels/trench roofs vanish; robot partly
  hidden under trench; ball *height* (airborne vs rolling) is unobservable — a real
  problem for shooting feedback.
- **Sim-to-real:** none for an onboard policy (no ceiling cam at a real event). Best
  used as **privileged critic input** (asymmetric actor-critic) or for debugging, not as
  the actor's observation.
- **Half-field variant (not rendered):** eye (0, ±4.1, 8), same look-down — doubles the
  pixels-per-ball (~9 px) and keeps your alliance's hub + neutral zone in frame; the
  right choice if an overhead observation is ever used for the actor-side in a
  fixed-half task curriculum.

### 5. hub_pov — from the red hub face toward the field
- **Observes:** the robot as seen from the goal (aim-error from the *target's* side),
  incoming ball arcs, neutral-zone fuel distribution.
- **Occlusion failures:** severe — any robot occludes everything behind it; the far half
  of the field is tiny (robot at 4.5 m is ~40 px); covers effectively one half-field.
- **Sim-to-real:** a fixed field camera is not available to the robot at runtime.
  Marginal value even for a critic (overhead dominates it). **Skip.**

### Dual-camera option: onboard_intake + onboard_shooter
The two onboard views are near-perfectly complementary: intake covers 0.4-6 m floor
(collection), shooter covers aim + skyline (scoring + localization). A 2×(3×128×128)
stacked observation covers every task-relevant state a real legacy could sense, at exactly
2× the render bill. This is the endgame rig; start with shooter-only if budget-bound.

## Render cost & parallel-RL verdict (RTX 5080 laptop, sm_120)

Measured on this scene (512×512, RaytracedLighting, one regular render product,
20-frame steady-state averages — `docs/cameras/timings_ms.json`):

- baseline `app.update()` with **no** render product: **7.5 ms/frame**
- with one render product attached: **10.0-10.8 ms/frame** across all five cameras
  → **≈ 2.8 ms/frame marginal render cost per 512² camera**

**TiledCamera — the mechanism that makes camera RL cheap (all envs batched into one
render pass) — hangs on sm_120**, so every camera is a full regular render product
rendered serially:
- 64 envs × 1 cam ≈ 64 × 2.8 ms ≈ **0.18 s of pure rendering per synchronized policy
  step** → ~5.5 policy steps/s, ~350 env-steps/s aggregate. 256 envs: ~0.72 s/step,
  same ~350 env-steps/s ceiling. A dual-cam rig halves that again.
- That assumes 64-256 render products stay resident; each allocates O(100 MB+) of RTX
  buffers, which does not fit in 16 GiB alongside PhysX — creating/destroying products
  per step is far slower still.
- State-based PhysX on this GPU runs the same 64-256 envs at 10⁴-10⁵ env-steps/s,
  i.e. vision-in-the-loop is ~2 orders of magnitude slower end-to-end.

**Verdict: pixels-in-the-loop RL at 64-256 envs is not viable on this machine.**
Strategy (in order):
1. **Train state-based first** (ball positions, robot pose, hub geometry as vectors) —
   this hardware's strength; get the scoring policy working.
2. **Distill to vision offline:** roll out the state-based teacher, render
   onboard_shooter(+intake) frames for the visited states one render-product-at-a-time
   (this survey script is the template), behavior-clone a vision student. Rendering is
   off the RL critical path, so sm_120's serial-render limit only costs wall-clock.
3. **Vision fine-tune on different hardware** (any GPU where TiledCamera works, e.g.
   cloud A10/L40/H100) or via an nvblox-style workaround (cheap depth/segmentation
   renders or state-constructed pseudo-images instead of full RTX RGB).

### Caveat for future vision work
The 32 AprilTags are currently **flat placeholder quads** (uniform `PALETTE["apriltag"]`
color, no 36h11 textures) — tag-based localization cannot be trained or validated until
real tag textures are applied to those quads.

## Ranked recommendation

1. **onboard_shooter** — the single camera to bet on: sees the hub when aiming (the
   scoring signal), retains floor-ball visibility beyond 1.5 m, matches a real
   Limelight mount, and keeps AprilTags in its band for real-world pose.
2. **onboard_intake + onboard_shooter (dual rig)** — the full-task observation; adopt as
   soon as the render/distillation budget allows 2 views.
3. **overhead_field** — as *privileged critic observation* (asymmetric actor-critic) and
   debugging; never as the deployed actor's input. Half-field variant if used actively.
4. **chase** — debug videos and qualitative evals only.
5. **hub_pov** — skip; dominated by overhead for every use case.
